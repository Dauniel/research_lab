Condensate Concepts - Quick Notes
==================================

WHAT ARE CONDENSATES
---------------------
Membrane-less organelles inside cells formed by liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS).
Past a critical concentration, molecules spontaneously demix into two coexisting phases:
  - Dense phase: droplet enriched in the molecule (the condensate)
  - Dilute phase: surrounding environment with low concentration
Analogous to oil droplets in water — distinct, sharp boundary, no membrane needed.


WHY THEY CLUSTER
-----------------
Driven by multivalent molecular interactions — when a molecule can bind multiple
neighbors simultaneously, they collectively collapse into a dense droplet.
It's a competition between:
  - Entropy: favors staying mixed (disorder)
  - Interactions: favorable bonds between molecules favor clustering
When interactions are strong enough (or concentration high enough), interactions win
-> phase separation occurs.


CONDENSATES AND RNA NANOSTARS
-------------------------------
RNA nanostars are the molecules that drive condensate formation in the Franco lab.
They are ~100-200 nt single strands that fold into multi-arm star shapes via stem-loops.
The tips of each arm have kissing loop sequences that bind to other nanostars' loops.
Because each nanostar has multiple arms (multivalent), one molecule can bind several neighbors at once -> cooperative clustering -> phase separation into condensates.
The condensate IS the dense droplet of many nanostars bound together.

SIGNIFICANCE AND PRACTICALITY:
---------------------	-------------
 Why it matters scientifically
  - Natural condensates (like stress granules, nucleolus) are hard to study because
  they're made of many different proteins and RNAs — it's hard to isolate cause and effect
  - RNA nanostars give you a minimal, programmable model — you control exactly what's in
  the condensate and tune its properties by sequence alone
  - This lets you ask clean questions: what controls condensate size? viscosity? nuclear
  vs. cytoplasmic localization?

  Practical applications being explored
  - Drug delivery — condensates could act as programmable depots that release cargo (RNA
  or protein) under specific cellular conditions
  - Recruiting specific RNAs — the paper shows nanostars can pull a target RNA into a
  condensate using a complementary hybridization domain, without modifying the target at
  all. In principle applicable to any transcript
  - Synthetic organelles — build a compartment inside a cell that concentrates specific
  molecules, mimicking what natural organelles do but with user-defined composition
  - Studying disease — many diseases (ALS, FTD, cancer) involve condensate dysfunction. A
  clean synthetic model helps understand the physics of how condensates go wrong


WHY GCGCG IS STRONGER
-----------------------
Kissing loop strength depends on the hydrogen bonds between base pairs:
  - G-C pairs form 3 hydrogen bonds
  - A-U pairs form only 2 hydrogen bonds
GCGCG is all G-C pairs -> maximum hydrogen bonds -> stronger, more stable interaction.
Weaker variants (e.g. UAUAUA) have A-U pairs -> fewer bonds -> easier to come apart.
Stronger kissing loops = higher partition coefficient, more viscous condensates,
more nuclear retention.


ARM LENGTH -> MORE OR LESS NUCLEAR CONDENSATES
------------------------------------------------
Longer arms = MORE nuclear condensates.
Arm length controls how fast nanostars can exit the nucleus through nuclear pores.
Longer arms = physically larger molecule = diffuses more slowly through the pore.
Slower export means nanostars accumulate in the nucleus long enough to condense there.
  - 10-nt arms: small, exit quickly -> cytoplasm only
  - 15-nt arms: at the threshold -> both nucleus and cytoplasm (JABr)
  - 20-25-nt arms: mostly nuclear


MORE ARMS -> MORE OR LESS NUCLEAR CONDENSATES
-----------------------------------------------
More arms = MORE nuclear condensates (indirectly).
Arm number controls the rate of nuclear aggregation (not export rate).
More arms = higher valency = each nanostar can bind more neighbors simultaneously
-> nanostars cluster together faster inside the nucleus before they can be exported.
Also increases small puncta in cytoplasm (higher nucleation there too).


WHY BROCCOLI = GREEN FLUORESCENCE
-----------------------------------
Broccoli is an RNA aptamer — a short RNA sequence that folds into a specific 3D shape
and binds a small molecule dye called DFHBI.
When Broccoli binds DFHBI, the complex fluoresces green.
It is named "Broccoli" because it glows green (like the vegetable).
It belongs to a family of fluorescent RNA aptamers: Spinach, Broccoli, Mango, Pepper.
These work like GFP but for RNA — instead of fusing a fluorescent protein, you attach
an RNA sequence (Broccoli) that activates a dye's fluorescence when folded correctly.
In JABr: the nanostar carries the Broccoli aptamer, so wherever JABr RNA concentrates
(inside condensates), you see bright green fluorescence signal.
