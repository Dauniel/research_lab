
Franco Lab Paper Notes
======================
"Programmable artificial RNA condensates in mammalian cells"
Li, Kim, Wang, Payson, Tang, Villalba Nieto, Osmanovic, Yang, Dilao, Bermudez, Xiao, Li, Lin, Plath, Black, Franco
bioRxiv, January 30, 2026
DOI: https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.01.28.702393


OVERVIEW
--------
The lab's flagship paper. Demonstrates that short single-stranded RNA strands can be
engineered to form programmable condensates inside living mammalian cells, with tunable
localization, composition, and material properties — all controlled by sequence design alone.


THE RNA NANOSTAR MOTIF
-----------------------
- ssRNA nanostars: ~100-200 nt single strands that fold into multi-arm star shapes via stem-loops
- Arms interact through kissing loops (palindromic loop-loop binding), driving phase separation
- Expressed in cells using the TORNADO system (U6 promoter + autocatalytic circularization for stability)
- Fluorescent reporters: Broccoli (green, DFHBI), Pepper (red, HBC620), Mango (yellow, TO1-B)
- Cell lines: HEK293T (main), HeLa, U-2 OS


DESIGN RULES FOR CONTROLLING CONDENSATES
------------------------------------------
Three parameters tune condensate localization, size, and number:

1. ARM LENGTH (controls nuclear export rate)
   - 10-nt arms (~3.4 nm, 56.8 kDa) → cytoplasm only (diffuse quickly through nuclear pore)
   - 15-nt arms (~5.1 nm, 66.3 kDa) → both nucleus and cytoplasm (pore permeability threshold)
   - 20-nt and 25-nt arms → both compartments, faster condensation, more nuclear retention
   - Longer arms = slower export = more nuclear condensates 
   - Longer arms are larger and diffuse more slowly through the nuclear pores

2. ARM NUMBER (valency) -> more kissing loop arms means each nanostar can form more simultaneous interactions with nanostars, so they cluster faster together in the nucleus

   - More arms → larger, fewer nuclear condensates; more small puncta in cytoplasm
   - Higher valency accelerates nuclear aggregation, slowing export
   - 2-arm to 4-arm range tested

3. KISSING LOOP STRENGTH (avidity)
   - Weakest: F variant (UAUAUA) → nuclear shells only, no full condensates
   - Medium: E variant (GUAUAC)
   - Standard: A variant (UCGCGA) — the JABr construct uses this
   - Strongest: WT variant (GCGCGC, HIV wild type) → largest nuclear condensates, highest viscosity
   - Stronger KL = higher nuclear retention + higher condensate viscosity

Summary: Nuclear condensate fraction increases with longer arms, more arms, stronger kissing loops.


CONDENSATE VOLUME DISTRIBUTIONS
---------------------------------
- Nuclear condensates: exponential volume distribution
- Cytoplasmic condensates: power-law (broader) distribution
- Explained by faster nanostar production in nucleus than export to cytoplasm
- Pattern holds across designs and time points


CONDENSATE MATERIAL PROPERTIES (FRAP + FUSION)
------------------------------------------------
- More viscous than P-granules and stress granules (which fuse in seconds)
- MCP-mCherry (guest protein) FRAP recovery: ~54s in cytoplasm, ~13.1s in nucleus
- Broccoli/Pepper aptamer FRAP dominated by ligand exchange, not RNA diffusion
- Pepper (Broccoli alternative) fitted time constant: 79.5s — lower diffusivity
- Incomplete recovery across all reporters → partially solid-like behavior
- Fusion timescales comparable to nucleolus; nuclear events faster than cytoplasmic
- One "splitting" event observed — may reflect interaction with cellular components


INTERACTIONS WITH NUCLEAR ORGANELLES
--------------------------------------
- Nucleolus: fibrillarin accumulates on condensate surface (excluded from interior)
- Cajal bodies: coilin aggregates on surface
- Stress granules (G3BP1): colocalization only in cells with high cytoplasmic RNA — likely
  metabolic stress from elevated RNA levels, not direct interaction
- G3BP1 wraps around shells (not condensates) in some nuclei
- No colocalization with P-bodies or nuclear speckles
- No immune response: IFN-beta, ISG15, IFIT1 levels comparable to control cells


MULTICOMPONENT / PROGRAMMABLE CONDENSATES
-------------------------------------------
- Distinct non-interacting condensates can coexist without mixing (kissing loops A, B, C)
- Each KL variant engages in homomeric but not heteromeric binding
- Aptamer choice (Broccoli vs. Pepper vs. Mango) affects folding and thus nuclear/cytoplasmic ratio

Linker nanostars (chimeric — carries two different KLs):
- 2-arm linkers: smaller, faster nuclear export → mixing at lower ratios in cytoplasm
- 4-arm linkers: larger, slower nuclear export → higher ratios needed for cytoplasmic mixing
- Mixing level controlled by stoichiometry of linker plasmid relative to distinct nanostar plasmids
- Colocalization quantified via Pearson correlation coefficient (PCC) and mixing indices


CARGO RECRUITMENT
------------------
Proteins:
- MS2 aptamer arm recruits MCP-mCherry reporter
- mCherry stays mostly cytoplasmic (no NLS)
- FRAP: stronger KL (WT vs. A) → slower mCherry recovery → higher condensate viscosity

Target RNA (sequence-specific, no tag required):
- Nanostar modified with 21-nt hybridization domain complementary to target RNA
- Colocalization observed only when sequences match
- Works without modifying the target RNA — applicable in principle to any transcript
- Longer recruitment domain slightly reduces condensate formation (steric hindrance)
  but can be mitigated by increasing nanostar:target ratio


SIMPLE COMPARTMENT MODEL
--------------------------
- Nanostars produced in nucleus at rate alpha, exported to cytoplasm at rate k_e
- k_e sensitive to nanostar size/design (nuclear pore permeability)
- Condensates form when concentration exceeds critical value
- Model explains why different designs produce nuclear-only, cytoplasmic-only, or both

BACKGROUND CONCEPTS
--------------------

LIQUID-LIQUID PHASE SEPARATION (LLPS)
- Normally molecules in a cell are spread out uniformly (like salt dissolved in water)
- Past a critical concentration threshold, molecules spontaneously demix into two coexisting phases:
    Dense phase: droplet enriched in the molecule (the condensate)
    Dilute phase: surrounding environment with low concentration
- Analogous to oil droplets forming in water — no membrane needed, purely interaction-driven
- Driven by competition between:
    Entropy: favors staying mixed (disorder)
    Interactions: favorable bonds between molecules favor clustering
- When interactions are strong enough (or concentration high enough), interactions win → phase separation
- Critical concentration: the threshold at which this occurs (referenced explicitly in the paper)
- RNA nanostars drive LLPS through kissing loop interactions — multivalency (multiple arms) allows
  one molecule to bind several neighbors simultaneously, collapsing into a dense droplet
- Partition coefficient measures phase separation strength: how much more concentrated is the
  RNA inside the condensate (dense phase) vs. outside (dilute phase)
  Reference PC = 6.32 means dense phase is ~6x more concentrated than dilute phase

ROLE OF CATIONS (SALT)
- RNA is heavily negatively charged — every nucleotide has a phosphate group on its backbone
- A single nanostar (~100-200 nt) carries a large negative charge
- Two RNA strands approaching each other experience strong electrostatic repulsion before
  kissing loop interactions can form
- Cations (Na+, K+, Mg2+) cluster around the negative backbone, partially neutralizing it
  → electrostatic screening allows strands to get close enough to bind
- Without sufficient cations: RNA strands repel, kissing loops can't form, no condensation

Divalent vs. monovalent cations:
- Mg2+ is especially important: 2+ charge makes it far more effective at screening than Na+/K+
- Mg2+ also directly stabilizes RNA secondary structure (stem-loops, kissing loops) by
  coordinating with the phosphate backbone — required for correct RNA folding
- Most in vitro RNA experiments require Mg2+ for this reason
- Salt concentration tunes interaction strength → affects critical concentration for phase separation
- In vivo: intracellular conditions (~150 mM K+, ~1 mM Mg2+) are set by the cell and sufficient
  for condensate formation in TORNADO-expressed nanostars

ARM NUMBER — WHAT RATE DOES IT AFFECT?
- More arms increases the rate of nuclear aggregation (condensation/nucleation), NOT export rate
- Arm length controls export rate (k_e) — physically larger molecules diffuse slower through the pore
- More arms = higher valency = each nanostar can form more simultaneous kissing loop interactions
  → nanostars cluster together faster in the nucleus
- Indirect effect on localization: nanostars aggregate into condensates before they can be exported,
  resulting in higher nuclear retention
- Also increases nucleation in cytoplasm (more small puncta) since valency enhances nucleation there too
